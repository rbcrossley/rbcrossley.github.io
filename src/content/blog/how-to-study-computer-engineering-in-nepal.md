# How to get good marks in Computer Engineering in Nepal?

I used to believe that learning in depth equals getting good marks. It does not. Tactical learning gets grades. You need to be highly strategic in learning. As I mentioned in my earlier blog

https://berojgarengineer.com/blog/computer-engineering-bsc-csit-career-guidance-for-plus-two-graduates/

> I know this because my batchmates who are now doing PhDs in reputed American universities never ever opened an authoritative textbook.

A few rules that you should follow to get good marks are as follows:

## Solve Question Papers
Solve all the questions that were asked in the last five years. There will be 50-60 questions per subject. There will be 6 subjects per semester. So you will need to solve 300 questions. 

I have seen some idiots copying from somewhere and claiming to solve the questions. This is not a correct way to learn. You have to solve the questions as if you were going to study them before the exam while being able to actually understand them.

Learning and solving questions take a huge amount of time.

But here is the mistake that students like me make. They get stuck in an old question loop. They keep solving every old question to date. But IOE does not repeat 100% of questions from the old question bank. Only 80% of the questions are repeated exactly from past questions.

So comes my next tip!

## Follow syllabus
Nowadays, it is really easy. The Loksewa exam that I am preparing for has a vague syllabus and few past questions. The way I am tackling it is through LLMs. I feed the past questions and syllabus to LLMs, ask them to generate 10-20 questions per subject, and practice those questions. If I finish early, I will again ask LLMs to provide newer questions and practice those questions. I do not merely practice the question that the LLM gives. But understand the precursor topics around that question, till that question. 

You can do the same. Feed the syllabus and past questions to LLMs. Then ask them to give questions that are not yet asked in old questions but could be asked in examinations. LLMs will read the syllabus and find such questions.

## Revision
I learnt this too late in my engineering degree. Your marks do not come from you studying something for the first time for 10 hours. They come from revisions. The more you revise, the more valuable content you will prepare for writing in the exam, and the more marks you will secure.

The best technique to revise is active recall. Active recall means being able to write the answer without looking at it. This is obviously an energy-consuming activity, and many students just re-read their notes to revise. But if you follow the active recall method of revision, you will be a highly successful student in Nepal's engineering degree.

# Time Management Techniques
One thing that wastes so much time of curious engineering students is "digging too deep".

Give yourself a deadline. Be it for a programming problem, an assignment problem, a past exam problem, or a project. Say "If I do not complete this programming task within 2 hours, I will seek help." Do not get stuck too much. It is not worth it. It is a kind of slippery slope. 

To be honest, if you follow all the techniques I am saying in this blog since the first semester, you will never get significantly stuck anywhere. Being stuck comes from a lack of prerequisite knowledge, mainly.

This is easier said than done. For me, when I get stuck, I feel an insane feeling that I should finish it by hook or crook. 

Another way to manage your time is to work deeply. In the age of distractions, it is very hard to work deeply. For example, most of you listen to songs or podcasts while solving assignments. This is not called studying deeply.

If you are not studying deeply, you will need to spend some extra time studying. One thing that really helped me with deep studying is visiting libraries. 

Since you are in college, you already have your college library. Study there every day. If it is open on weekends, it is great. Go there and study all day. If not, there are some public libraries that you could visit, like the Kaiser Library, the Central Law Library, etc. I recommended these libraries because you can take your books and study in these libraries.

If you are looking for paid study libraries, there are various "study rooms" that charge around Rs.3500/month. They are insanely helpful. But I believe you, as a student, do not need such expensive libraries. Just visit your college library. Ask the coordinator to make college libraries open on weekends as well. You will see a rapid increase in your grades when you start studying in the library. Trust me on this.

# How to really learn new and technical subjects

Say you have to learn distributed systems, which is a highly rigorous subject.
First, ensure you have all necessary prerequisites for the course cleared.
Then, follow the study techniques:
- interleaving+pomodoro
- active recall(revising)
- deadline time for a problem
- study tactically

New subjects can take a good amount of time to learn. If you are stuck today, just sleep after reading a little bit of that topic tonight. Read that topic tomorrow morning when you wake up. And keep repeating this till you learn. # Daily study routine for Nepalese engineering students

There are simple rules of thumb that you should follow. For example, for every 1 credit hour, you should be studying 3 hours at home. Do not consider tuition classes or tutorial classes in this. Just the lectures.

As a full-time student, you should aim for 50 hours of studies per week.

How can it be achieved?

- 8 hours of sleep
- 8 hours of college
- 6 hours of self-studies
- 2 hours for other stuffs

So during weekdays, you will have 30 hours of self-study. on weekends, you should do 10 hours each day. It is difficult to be real. I do not lie. And many students do not follow this schedule. Since the engineering degree in Nepal is not as rigorous as a foreign university's degree, you might not even need this. But if you genuinely want to do everything in engineering, this is very necessary. By genuinely, I mean:
- not copying lab reports
- not copying assignments
- not copying projects
